# SafeAI.sg Advisory Council Charter
**Centre of Excellence for AI Technical Safety & Governance**
*Founding Document · Version 1.0 · Singapore · 2026*

---

## Preamble

Singapore's digital economy is entering a decisive phase. Artificial intelligence is no longer a horizon technology — it is active infrastructure. AI agents are being woven into enterprise workflows, public sector operations, financial services, healthcare delivery, and critical national systems. The pace of deployment is outrunning the organisational capacity to govern it well.

The risk is not that organisations will fail to adopt AI. The risk is that they will adopt it in ways that are either:

- **Under-governed** — exposing enterprises, customers, and society to avoidable harm; or
- **Over-governed** — applying compliance processes designed for high-stakes scenarios as blanket policy across low-risk applications, creating friction that stalls innovation and drives AI initiatives underground.

SafeAI.sg is established to close this gap. It is Singapore's **industry-led Centre of Excellence for AI technical safety and practical governance** — a platform where CIOs, CISOs, CTOs, and their teams can access calibrated, use-case-specific guidance that allows them to deploy AI with confidence, at the appropriate level of rigour, across the full spectrum of risk.

## Part I — Mission and Purpose

### 1.1 Mission Statement

SafeAI.sg exists to make AI deployment **safer, more confident, and more economically rational** for Singapore-based enterprises, public agencies, and technology teams — by providing:

- A risk-weighted, use-case-calibrated governance framework
- A curated library of technical controls
- A trusted peer community of senior AI practitioners

We build the practical infrastructure of AI governance: the scorecards, the control playbooks, the observability references, and the community of practice that enable organisations to answer two questions for every AI initiative:

1. What is the right level of governance for this specific use case?
2. What are the specific controls, processes, and tools that implement that level of governance across every system this AI touches?

### 1.2 The Three Pillars of SafeAI.sg

SafeAI.sg delivers its mission through three distinct but mutually reinforcing pillars.

#### Pillar 1 — Open Risk Assessment Toolkit and Governance Controls Standard

SafeAI.sg will develop, maintain, and freely publish a practitioner-validated, self-service risk assessment and governance controls toolkit as an open online resource, accessible at no cost to any Singapore organisation. The centrepiece is the **Universal AI Use-Case Risk Score (UARS)** — a structured, five-dimension scoring instrument that any technology team can apply to any AI use case to determine the appropriate level of governance required, without needing external consultants or specialist resources.

The toolkit provides:
- A risk score (UARS)
- Commensurate governance controls per risk band
- Implementation processes to deploy those controls
- A policy-as-code extensibility mechanism ensuring policies propagate consistently across every system the AI touches

The toolkit is published as an **open standard** — not proprietary intellectual property — so it can be adopted, referenced, and built upon freely across Singapore's enterprise and public sector ecosystem.

#### Pillar 2 — Technology Showcase and Associate Programme

SafeAI.sg operates an **Associate Programme** for technology companies — including AI platform providers, cybersecurity vendors, observability tooling companies, and governance technology firms — to demonstrate how strong AI governance controls can be embedded into real-world enterprise AI deployments.

Associates may showcase through community events, reference architecture demonstrations, and case study publications, illustrating how their products address specific governance requirements at defined UARS tiers.

**Important:** SafeAI.sg does not test, validate, certify, or endorse any technology provided by Associate companies as compliant with any regulatory requirement or as meeting any defined governance standard. Organisations evaluating Associate technologies remain solely responsible for their own due diligence and compliance assessments.

#### Pillar 3 — Professional Certification and Skills Development

SafeAI.sg will co-develop a structured professional certification programme in partnership with Singapore's institutes of higher learning, spanning four levels — from career conversion for non-technical professionals through to senior certification for enterprise architects and governance leaders.

Governance frameworks are only sustainable when the people responsible for implementing them possess the knowledge to do so.

## Part II — Target Community and Stakeholders

**CISOs and Information Security Leaders**
Security leaders who must extend their governance models beyond classical cybersecurity to address the new attack surfaces introduced by AI agents, multi-agent systems, generative AI infrastructure, and autonomous workflows.

## Part IV — The Universal AI Use-Case Risk Score (UARS)

### The Governance Gap UARS Addresses

Existing AI governance frameworks — including the IMDA Model AI Governance Framework, the CSA Guidelines on Securing AI Systems, and sector-specific regulatory guidance — provide comprehensive principles. However, they leave a critical operational gap: they do not provide a single, use-case-level diagnostic that practitioners can apply quickly to determine which tier of governance is appropriate.

The result is governance decisions made subjectively and inconsistently — organisations either over-govern low-risk tools or under-govern high-risk deployments. Both failure modes are costly and avoidable.

### UARS as an Open, Self-Service Online Resource

The UARS framework will be published as a **freely accessible, self-service online resource**. Any organisation — regardless of size, sector, or technical sophistication — can access the UARS scoring instrument at no cost, without registration, without the need to engage SafeAI.sg or any third party.

The online resource will include:
- The UARS scoring worksheet with guided instructions
- The risk band reference table mapping scores to governance tiers
- Tier 1, 2, and 3 control playbooks with implementation process guidance
- The policy-as-code extensibility framework
- A library of worked examples across common AI use case categories
- A changelog documenting framework updates

### The Five Dimensions

UARS evaluates each AI use case across five weighted dimensions. Each dimension is scored 1 (Low), 2 (Medium), or 3 (High).

**Scoring formula:** UARS = (A × 2) + (B × 2) + (C × 1.5) + (D × 1.5) + (E × 1)
**Minimum: 7 · Maximum: 21**

| Dimension | Weight | Low (1) → High (3) |
|---|---|---|
| **A — Autonomy** | ×2 | Supervised decision support → Fully autonomous |
| **B — Data Sensitivity** | ×2 | Public data → PII/regulated/financial data |
| **C — Attack Surface** | ×1.5 | Fully internal → Internet-facing multi-agent mesh |
| **D — Consequentiality** | ×1.5 | Read-only actions → Irreversible external actions |
| **E — Error Tolerance** | ×1 | High domain tolerance → Zero-tolerance critical |

### Risk Bands and Governance Tiers

The UARS score places each use case into one of three risk bands. The risk band assignment is not a binary pass/fail — it is a calibration instrument. **UARS is a living score, not a one-time compliance gate.**

### Extensibility: The Policy-as-Code Principle

A governance framework is only as effective as its reach. Under the Policy-as-Code principle, guardrail rules, tool permission policies, HITL checkpoint specifications, and monitoring alert thresholds are encoded as **versioned, machine-readable policy artefacts** deployed through a centralised policy engine.

Any system that the AI use case touches inherits the relevant controls on deployment automatically. When a policy is updated, the update propagates automatically to all affected systems — eliminating governance drift.

## Part V — The Operational Governance Framework

### Governance Tier 1 — Standard AI Governance (UARS 7–10)

**Applies to:** Limited autonomy, read-only or sandboxed tool access, non-regulated data, fully internal attack surface, high error tolerance.

**Mandatory Controls:**
- LLM Guardrails and Output Filtering: Prompt injection defences, output classifiers, hallucination detection
- Agent Identity and Access Baseline: Unique agent identifiers, read-only tool permissions, full tool call logging

**Implementation Processes:**
- Policy-as-Code Review (Monthly): Guardrail rules encoded as versioned policies in the CI/CD pipeline
- Lightweight Adversarial Testing (Quarterly): Structured adversarial prompt library run against the production endpoint

### Governance Tier 2 — Agentic Governance (UARS 11–15)

**Applies to:** Meaningful autonomy, writes to internal systems, business-sensitive data, internal APIs and trusted external services.

**Mandatory Controls:**
- Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) Checkpoint Architecture: Action-boundary taxonomy requiring human approval for irreversible/high-stakes/outlier actions
- MCP Allowlisting and Tool Scope Enforcement: Approved MCP servers whitelisted, least-privilege tooling per agent role

**Implementation Processes:**
- Agentic Workflow Threat Model (Per Use Case, Pre-Deployment): Structured taint-trace session mapping all data flows
- Continuous Trajectory Monitoring with Alert Thresholds: Programmatic alert thresholds with automatic workflow pause

### Governance Tier 3 — Full AI Safety Stack (UARS 16–21)

**Applies to:** Fully autonomous agents, irreversible external actions (payments, communications, data deletion), regulated or PII-sensitive data, internet exposure, low error tolerance domains (financial services, healthcare, legal, critical infrastructure).

**Mandatory Controls:**
- AI Security Posture Management (AI-SPM) and Generative AI Firewall (GAF): Continuous model inventory, semantic AI firewall with dialogue-level intent analysis, PII redaction, and egress filtering
- Agentic Control Plane with Circuit Breakers: Runtime guardrails wrapping the full Reason → Plan → Tool → Memory loop; non-agentic fallback modes for all critical functions

**Implementation Processes:**
- Pre-Deployment Safety Case and Simulation Testing: Structured safety case with sign-off required from CISO and accountable business owner
- Continuous Red-Team and Incident Playbook Cadence (Monthly): Adversarial coverage including prompt injection, tool misuse, memory poisoning, MCP server compromise, and multi-agent cascading failure

## Part VI — Tool and Technology Reference Programme

### Purpose and Scope

A governance framework without practical tooling is aspirational rather than operational. SafeAI.sg maintains a curated reference library of technical tools, platforms, and architectural patterns providing the observability, control, enforcement, and audit capabilities required at each UARS tier.

### Tool Reference Categories

| Category | Capability |
|---|---|
| **Agentic Identity and Access Control** | Per-agent identity, dynamic permission scoping, OAuth 2.0 extensions for MCP, A2A protocol |
| **Generative AI Firewalls and Semantic Runtime Controls** | Prompt-level semantic analysis, intent classification, jailbreak detection, PII redaction, egress filtering |
| **AI Security Posture Management (AI-SPM)** | Continuous model inventory, model provenance tracking, supply chain risk assessment, configuration drift detection |
| **Agentic Workflow Observability** | Step-level tracing of agent reasoning, plan execution, tool calls, and memory operations |
| **DSPM for AI and Vector Store Security** | Vector database governance, RAG pipeline data flows, embedding provenance, PDPA / MAS TRM compliance |
| **Policy-as-Code Engines** | OPA-compatible versioned, machine-readable governance policies with auto-propagation |
| **Red-Team and Adversarial Testing Frameworks** | Prompt injection, agentic workflow stress testing, multi-agent cascading failure simulation, MITRE ATLAS alignment |

## Part VIII — Advisory Council Structure and Governance

### 8.1 Role of the Advisory Council

The SafeAI.sg Advisory Council is the primary **governance and direction-setting body** of the Centre. Its role is to provide strategic oversight, domain expertise, institutional credibility, and ongoing challenge to the Centre's frameworks, publications, and community programmes.

The Advisory Council is not an operational or executive body. **It does not approve, endorse, certify, or regulate.**

### 8.2 Advisory Council Composition

| Domain | Representation |
|---|---|
| Government and Public Policy | Government technology agencies and digital economy bodies |
| Cybersecurity and National Security | Singapore's cybersecurity ecosystem practitioners and institutional representatives |
| Financial Services and Regulated Industries | Senior practitioners from regulated sectors |
| Academic and Research Institutions | AI safety researchers and governance scholars |
| Enterprise Technology Leadership | Sitting CIOs, CTOs, and CISOs from large Singapore enterprises |
| International Partnerships | Representatives from peer AI safety bodies in UK, US, and Asia-Pacific |

### 8.3 Advisory Council Responsibilities

1. Provide strategic direction for SafeAI.sg's research agenda, framework development priorities, and community programmes
2. Review and provide substantive feedback on UARS methodology, risk band calibration, and control set recommendations at least annually
3. Identify emerging AI risk domains, new use case categories, and governance gaps
4. Promote SafeAI.sg frameworks within their organisations and sectors
5. Serve as a sounding board for SPARK on SafeAI.sg strategy, partnerships, and programme development
6. Support the maintenance of SafeAI.sg's independence by flagging any actual or potential conflicts of interest

### 8.4 Meeting Cadence

The full Advisory Council shall convene at **minimum twice per year** in a structured review session.

### 8.5 Independence and Conflict of Interest

Advisory Council membership does not create any commercial relationship between a member's organisation and SafeAI.sg. Members who have a material commercial interest in a specific tool, platform, or framework recommendation under discussion shall declare that interest and shall not participate in formal feedback on that matter.

## Part IX — Operating Principles

| Principle | Description |
|---|---|
| **Proportionality** | Governance should be commensurate with risk. No AI use case should bear more governance friction than its actual risk exposure warrants. |
| **Practicality** | Frameworks that remain on paper deliver no safety benefit. Every SafeAI.sg output shall be designed for operational implementation by a working technology team. |
| **Independence** | SafeAI.sg does not regulate, certify, or endorse. Its credibility rests on the quality of its analysis and the trust of its community. |
| **Inclusivity** | SafeAI.sg frameworks shall be designed to be accessible and applicable to mid-market enterprises, public agencies, and technology teams of all sizes. |
| **Continuous Improvement** | SafeAI.sg commits to a continuous update cycle for its UARS methodology, control recommendations, and tool reference library. |
| **Transparency** | SafeAI.sg's methodology, framework development process, and basis for risk band calibrations shall be publicly documented and open to challenge. |

## Part X — Professional Certification and Skills Development

### 10.1 Purpose

A governance framework is only sustainable when the people responsible for implementing it possess the knowledge and skills to do so with confidence. SafeAI.sg will co-develop a structured professional certification programme with **NUS, NTU, SMU, and SIT** — designed for working professionals, aligned to the SafeAI.sg governance framework and UARS methodology.

### 10.2 Certification Level Architecture

| Level | Title | Audience | Covers |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Level 0** | Career Conversion Certification | Non-technical professionals transitioning into AI governance | AI fundamentals & risk literacy, UARS framework orientation, Governance process basics |
| **Level 1** | SafeAI User & Citizen Builder | Business users, project managers, citizen developers | Applying UARS self-service toolkit, Tier 1 governance controls, Responsible AI usage patterns |
| **Level 2** | SafeAI Certified Engineer | AI/ML engineers, MLOps specialists, solution architects | Full UARS scoring methodology, Tiers 1–3 technical controls, Policy-as-code implementation |
| **Level 3** | SafeAI Certified Chief Architect | Senior AI architects, CISOs, governance leads | Enterprise AI safety architecture, Full AI safety stack design, Advisory & organisational governance |

*All levels include a structured continuing education requirement to maintain certification currency.*

## Part XI — Charter Administration

### 11.1 Charter Review

This Charter shall be reviewed by the Advisory Council at minimum annually and updated to reflect material changes in the Centre's scope, structure, stakeholder composition, or operating environment.

### 11.2 Amendments

Amendments may be proposed by the SPARK by CIO Academy Asia executive team or by any Advisory Council member. Proposed amendments shall be circulated to the full Advisory Council at least **30 days** before a formal meeting, and shall require the agreement of a **majority of Advisory Council members present** at that meeting to take effect.

### 11.3 Effective Date

This Charter takes effect upon adoption by the founding Advisory Council and shall remain in force until superseded by a subsequent version approved in accordance with the amendment process set out above.
